# Signs of Spring 
## Charting Seasonal Shifts Through Urban Wildlife in Philadelphia

Each year, as winter recedes, familiar animals return to Philadelphia — the Red-winged Blackbird’s trills pierce the still air, Robins hop across melting lawns, and Groundhogs emerge from hibernation. This project explores these species as **seasonal indicators**, proposing that urban wildlife can reflect ecological transitions.

## Interpretation and Context

This work falls within **phenology**, the study of how life cycles are timed with seasons. While climate change accelerates and shifts these cycles, local data can still reveal rich seasonal patterns. These species serve as accessible indicators for city dwellers to watch — grounded in science but visible without instruments.

Rather than relying solely on folklore (sorry, Phil), this project shows:
- **Birds respond to early warmth**
- **Groundhogs emerge once consistent warming arrives**
- **Wildlife patterns track real environmental cues**

## Conclusion

This project frames everyday animals as **phenological clues** — like stopwatches tuned to temperature. By tracking their appearances alongside weather data, we gain both insight and wonder: the changing seasons are written into the lives of our neighbors, feathered and furred.
